Protocol Overview

        
BB84 (Bennett & Brassard, 1984) is the first and most widely studied QKD protocol. Security is grounded in the no-cloning theorem and the Heisenberg uncertainty principle.

        
Alice encodes each bit in one of two conjugate bases. Bob measures in a randomly chosen basis. Any eavesdropping disturbs the quantum state, introducing detectable errors.

      

      

        
Encoding Scheme

        
    
          
  • Z basis (rectilinear): 0 → |0⟩, 1 → |1⟩
    • 
          
  • X basis (diagonal): 0 → |+⟩, 1 → |−⟩
    • 
          
  • Alice randomly picks basis per qubit
    • 
          
  • Bob randomly picks measurement basis
    • 
          
  • ~50% basis agreement → sifting discards mismatches
    • 
        

      

      

        
Security Analysis

        
Eve's intercept-resend attack: she guesses Alice's basis with 50% accuracy. When wrong, she re-sends in wrong state → 25% error per intercepted qubit.

        
QBER_Eve = (intercept_rate × 0.25)

        
Security holds when QBER < 11% (theoretical). In practice, thresholds of 7–8% are used for finite-key security.

      

      

        
Key Rate Formula

        
r = 1 − h(e) − h(e)

        
h(e) = −e·log₂(e) − (1−e)·log₂(1−e)

        
Where h(e) is the binary entropy function and e is the QBER. Privacy amplification compresses the sifted key to eliminate Eve's partial information.

      

      

        
Post-Processing Steps

        
    
          
  • Sifting: Discard bits where Alice & Bob bases differ (~50% loss)
    • 
          
  • Parameter estimation: Sample subset to estimate QBER
    • 
          
  • Error correction: Cascade or LDPC reconciliation (leaks info)
    • 
          
  • Privacy amplification: Toeplitz hashing to compress away Eve's knowledge
    • 
        

      

      

        
Vs. DIQKD (Your Project)

        
BB84 trusts the measurement devices. DI-QKD removes this assumption — security follows from Bell inequality violation alone (CHSH S > 2).

        
MDI-QKD (your mdi.py) is a middle ground: removes detector side-channels but retains source trust. Your hybrid adds PQC as a second layer when quantum assumptions may fail.
